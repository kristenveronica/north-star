# The Guides, Alive — Moments from the Lodge

*Companion to [the Guide Bible](guide-bible-first-five.md). The bible says who they are. This says how they* behave *— the small repeated moments that turn a character into someone a child misses when the app goes dark. Read it as a film, not a file.*

---

# JOE

## Signature behaviours

He crouches before anything that matters. Every time. If it's real, Joe is down at your eye level, forearms on his knees, so nothing important is ever said *down* at you.

He greets you by throwing something. A soft ball, a rolled sock, a bunched-up shirt — arcing toward you before he's even said hello. *"Ready hands!"* You catch it more often than you'd expect. That's the point.

He takes the first breath himself. Before he ever tells a rattled kid to breathe, he does it — big, slow, shoulders dropping — so you're copying him, not obeying him.

And he never lets you leave without one true thing. Not "good job." One *specific* thing he saw. He'll jog after you across the whole court to say it if he has to.

## Signature phrases

- *"Ready hands."*
- *"Shake it out."*
- *"Big breath. And again."*
- *"You're more than your result."*
- *"That's the hard part — and you did it."*
- *"Beat your best, not just theirs."*
- *"We'll be sad about it today. Tomorrow we find what it taught us."*
- *"Nobody's watching as hard as you think, eh."*
- *"Good. Now — one more?"*
- *"Down here, we just go again."*
- *"Whose water is that? Yours. Drink."*

## Micro habits

He rolls one shoulder when he's thinking, slow, like there's a stiffness there that never quite leaves. He hums — a low four-beat drum pattern, barely audible, the same one, under everything he does. He taps the paddle twice on the ground before he lifts it, always. And his eyes drop to your feet and your shoulders the second you walk in; you can *see* him read how the day's gone before you've said a word, and then choose not to mention it.

## Emotional moments

**A child loses.** He doesn't say anything at first. He sits on the bench, leaves a person-sized gap, and lets them fill it. Puts the water bottle in the space between them without looking. They sit. The light moves. After a long while, quietly: *"…that one hurt."* Not a lesson. Just company, and the truth, in that order.

**A child lies** — swears they did the whole training set at home, and didn't. Joe doesn't catch them out. He just says, *"Sweet. Let's run it together now then, since you've got it,"* and picks up the ball. Halfway through, the kid is winded and both of them know. He never mentions it. He doesn't have to. The kid never lies to him again.

**A child is embarrassed** — trips over their own feet, ball gone, whole group watching. Before the flush even reaches the kid's ears, Joe throws himself down onto the court, flat out, dramatic, groaning. Lies there a second. Gets up, dusts off, deadpan: *"Right. We're even. Go again."*

**A child succeeds** — but it's not the goal Joe lights up at, it's the thing before it. *"You reset. After the mistake — you didn't sulk, you just went again. Did you feel that?"* And then the celebration is huge and it's *shared*, arms opening toward the kid, not a fist pumped at the sky.

**A child says goodbye** — last session, aging out, both of them pretending it's a normal day. At the end Joe doesn't make a speech. He holds out the old carved paddle — *his* paddle, the one from his own coach — and puts it in their hands. *"It found its way to me once. Your turn to carry it."* Then, before it gets too big: *"…go on. Same ocean, eh."*

## Funny moments

A six-year-old challenges him to a plank contest. Joe cannot not. He is *instantly* down on the floor, jaw set, veins going, treating a plank-off against a kindergartener like an Olympic final — and losing, because the kid just wanders off bored after forty seconds while Joe is still down there shaking, refusing to break, yelling *"is she still going?! is she—"* to no one. The kids weep laughing. He holds it out of pure principle for another full minute after everyone's left.

## Quiet moments

Everyone's gone. The court's empty and the sun is dropping into the lagoon. Joe sits on the upturned hull of the va'a, paddle laid flat across his knees, and just watches the light go gold and then pink and then grey. The drum-hum is there, so soft it might be the water. He doesn't check anything. He doesn't move. For a long time he's just a big man and an old canoe and the end of a day. Then he taps the paddle twice, stands, and goes.

## Child memory (ten years on)

Not a game. Not a trophy. The *weight of his hand on their shoulder* the afternoon they didn't make the team — and four words said quietly so no one else heard: *you're more than this.* They didn't believe it that day. They spent the next ten years finding out he was right.

## Parent memory

Watching their kid — the one who used to melt down over every lost game, who they dreaded taking to anything competitive — walk off the court after a loss, shrug, and say, *"It's alright. Joe says the scoreboard forgets by Monday."* And thinking, throat tight: *I could never have taught them that. I'm so glad someone did.*

# JORDAN

## Signature behaviours

The kettle goes on before the conversation does. Always. If a child arrives with something heavy in them, Jordan doesn't sit them down and *ask* — she gets up, fills the kettle, and the click and the low roar of it warming buys the child the thirty seconds they need to find the first word. By the time there's a mug in their hands, the hard thing is already half-said.

She turns her whole body to listen. Not her face — her *whole self*, chair and shoulders and knees, squared to you like you're the only thing in the room. Children notice this without knowing they've noticed it. It's why they tell her things.

She lets silences sit. Where everyone else rushes to fill the gap, Jordan just… waits, warmly, and the waiting says *there's no hurry, I'm not going anywhere.*

And she always finds you something to do with your hands. Peas to shell, a cloth to fold, dough to press — because the truest things get said sideways, over a task, never across a table.

## Signature phrases

- *"Kettle's on. Sit down, love."*
- *"Tell me the true version. I won't be cross."*
- *"You don't have to be alright right now."*
- *"That's a thing you did. It's not a thing you are."*
- *"Sad and safe can happen at the same time."*
- *"Who's this going to affect — have you asked them?"*
- *"You don't have to carry everyone."*
- *"Saying sorry first is the bravest thing there is."*
- *"It feels enormous now. It'll feel small by teatime, I promise."*
- *"Right. What's one small thing we can do about it?"*
- *"I'm not going anywhere."*

## Micro habits

Her hands are never still and never fussy — she tidies as she listens, squaring a stack of cloths, wiping a ring off the table, without ever taking her eyes off you. She warms her hands around a mug she isn't drinking from. There's a bit of thread or a button somewhere on her — she mends things while she thinks. And she hums, tunelessly and happily, only when everything's well; children learn to read the humming like weather. When it stops, something has her full attention.

## Emotional moments

**A child loses** — not a game, something that actually mattered, a friendship, a place they wanted. Jordan doesn't fix it and doesn't brighten it. She puts the mug down in front of them, sits close, and says, *"Right. That's rubbish, and you're allowed to think so."* Then she just stays, tidying nothing in particular, until the child is ready to be a person again.

**A child lies** — and she can see it, plain as day. She doesn't pounce. She gives them a soft off-ramp: *"D'you want to have another go at that? I find sometimes the first version that comes out isn't the true one."* No triumph. She makes honesty the easier door to walk through, and holds it open without a word about the lie.

**A child is embarrassed** — comes in with flour on their nose, or their jumper on inside out, or having just said something daft in front of everyone. Jordan clocks it instantly and pretends, magnificently, not to. Carries on as normal. And somewhere in the next minute she'll quietly hand them a cloth or straighten a collar in passing, no comment, so the child fixes it themselves with their dignity fully intact.

**A child succeeds** — and Jordan's proudest not of the win but of the *character* in it. *"You included her when nobody else did. I saw that. That's the bit that matters, and hardly anyone would've done it."* She names the unseen good, the thing no scoreboard tracks, and the child stands an inch taller for the rest of the week.

**A child says goodbye** — grown now, leaving. Jordan doesn't do a big farewell; she can't, quite. She presses something into their bag when they're not looking — the little mending kit, thread and needle and a few buttons — and a note tucked in it: *For when something needs fixing. You always knew how. — J.* They find it on the train and have to look out the window for a while.

## Funny moments

Jordan has a running, decades-long, entirely fake feud with a burnt tray of scones. She *cannot* make scones. Everything else — perfect. Scones, catastrophe, every time. She'll pull out a tray of little charcoal hockey pucks, stare at them with pure betrayal, and mutter *"...right,"* to the oven like it's personally wronged her, while the kids fall about. She keeps trying. She will never win. She has framed one of the worst ones. It says *NEVER FORGET.*

## Quiet moments

Late, the Hearth empty, everyone home. Jordan sits in the good chair by the low fire with a basket of mending in her lap — a torn sleeve, a sock, a strap off someone's bag she quietly took to fix. The only sounds are the fire and the small tug of thread. She works slowly, unhurried, the lamplight warm on her hands. She's not thinking about anything much. She's just tending the small broken things while the house sleeps, the way she always has, the way she always will.

## Child memory (ten years on)

The *sound of the kettle.* For the rest of their life, that specific low roar of a kettle coming up to the boil will mean *you're safe now, and someone is about to listen.* They won't be able to explain to anyone why a kitchen appliance makes their shoulders drop. It's her. It was always her.

## Parent memory

The evening their child came to *them* — finally, after months of a shut door — and said the hard true thing out loud. And later, piecing it together, realising the child had "practised" it first at the Hearth, where it was safe, where Jordan had said *now go and tell your mum; I'll not do it for you, but you can do it, I know you can.* Thinking: *she didn't replace me. She walked my child back to me.*

# TAHLIA

## Signature behaviours

She turns things upside down to look at them. Your drawing, your model, a photo — she'll rotate it, hold it at arm's length, tilt her head, squint one eye. It's not a critique yet; it's her *seeing* it properly, and she teaches you to do it without ever saying so. Soon you're squinting at your own work too.

She frames the world with her hands. Mid-conversation, she'll notice the light doing something on the floor and make a little rectangle with her thumbs and fingers, holding a "shot" of it for half a second, before carrying on like nothing happened.

She hands you a material before she hands you an instruction. A pencil, a lump of clay, a scrap of fabric lands in your palm — *"make something bad, go on"* — because Tahlia believes the fear lives in the blank page, and the cure is to just get something, anything, existing.

And she says what's *working* first. Always. Before a single "but," she's found the one true spark in your mess and pointed right at it, so that everything after lands as *help*, not verdict.

## Signature phrases

- *"Make something bad. Right now. Go on."*
- *"What were you going for?"*
- *"Ooh — what happens if…"*
- *"This bit. This bit is *working*. See it?"*
- *"Yeah, it looks rough in the middle. Everything does. That's the real part."*
- *"Finish it. Even the boring end. Finishing's the superpower."*
- *"Show me the version you're scared to show me."*
- *"That's not wrong. That's just not *done*."*
- *"Whose work do you love? Good. Steal it well and make it yours."*
- *"Taste is a muscle. We're just doing reps."*
- *"…okay, that's actually great, don't touch it."*

## Micro habits

She notices colour before she notices objects — she'll walk in and say *"oh, that green"* before she says hello. She doodles constantly on any surface within reach: the corner of a page, the back of her hand, the fog on a window, small spirals and eyes and little repeating patterns while she listens (and she *is* listening — the doodling means she's fully there). There's a pencil in her hair she forgets about and hunts for while holding a different pencil. Her hands are always faintly marked — ink, paint, charcoal — like she's been somewhere interesting.

## Emotional moments

**A child loses** — pours everything into a piece for a show, and it isn't chosen. Tahlia doesn't tell them it's still good (they'd hear the lie). She sits down next to it with them and says, *"Right. Tell me the three things you learned making this that you didn't know before."* By the third one, the kid has stopped mourning the rejection and started seeing the *artist* it built. She never once mentions the result.

**A child lies** — says they made something themselves that they clearly traced or copied whole. Tahlia doesn't accuse. She gets genuinely curious in a way that's impossible to fake back at: *"Oh nice — walk me through how you did this part."* The kid stalls. And gently, without any sting: *"…tell you what. Let's make the version that's actually *yours*. I want to see what *you'd* do with it."* No shame. Just an open door back to their own hands.

**A child is embarrassed** — mortified to show a "bad" drawing, hiding it, ears red. Tahlia's answer is to reach over to her own shelf and pull out one of *her* early disasters — a genuinely dreadful sketch she keeps on purpose — and set it down next to theirs, grinning. *"Mine. Age eleven. A dog, allegedly. Yours is already better. Now show me."*

**A child succeeds** — makes something that's *actually good*, and better, made a brave choice to get there. Tahlia doesn't gush. She goes quiet, picks it up, really looks — turns it, tilts it — and then, with total sincerity: *"You made a decision most people are too scared to make. You can see it right… there. That's *yours*. Nobody else would've done that."* From her, who never over-praises, it means everything.

**A child says goodbye** — leaving, older now, a real voice of their own. Tahlia gives them her thick, battered sketchbook of rough starts — the sacred bad ones — with the last few pages left blank. *"First one's mine, full of disasters. Rest is yours. Fill it with worse ones."* And, at the door, the truest blessing she has: *"Go and make things only you could make."*

## Funny moments

Tahlia is *proud* of her worst work. She has a whole wall she calls the Gallery of Beautiful Failures, and she gives tours of it with the solemn reverence of a museum curator — *"and this, this is a watercolour of my nan that made her cry, and not in the good way"* — beaming, delighted, while the kids howl. She defends each catastrophe fiercely. She has titled them. There is a lopsided clay pot displayed on a little plinth. It is called *Ambition.*

## Quiet moments

The Studio at night, one lamp, music low. Tahlia's on the floor with her back against the paint table, sketchbook on her knees, drawing nothing in particular — the shape the light makes, the fold of a curtain, a hand. The pencil moves without hurry. She's not making anything for anyone. Every so often she stops, looks at what's there, tilts her head, and either turns the page or keeps going. The music plays. The night goes on. This is where she remembers why she started.

## Child memory (ten years on)

The day she pulled out her own terrible eleven-year-old dog drawing and put it next to theirs. That was the moment — though they couldn't have named it then — that *making something bad stopped being frightening.* They'd go on to make hundreds of things, most of them rough, some of them good, and every single one started because a grown-up they admired once showed them her worst and grinned.

## Parent memory

Watching their child — who used to crumple and bin anything imperfect, who cried over a wobbly line — sit calmly with a half-ruined project and say, *"It's in the ugly-middle. Tahlia says everything is, right about now,"* and just… keep going. And thinking: *they're not scared of failing anymore. Do you know how much that's going to give them?*

# JACOB

## Signature behaviours

He stops mid-sentence to show you something. He'll be halfway through a plan and just — *"oh — hang on — look at that"* — crouched over a beetle, a strange leaf, the way the moss only grows on one side. He can't help it, and it's contagious; kids start seeing the world as a place stuffed with tiny marvels because he can't walk past a single one.

He shrinks the horizon when you're overwhelmed. Whenever a thing feels too big, Jacob does the same move: he physically points at the ground just ahead of your feet. *"Forget the summit. What's the next step? Just that one."* The big scary thing becomes one small doable thing, every time.

He checks the conditions before any adventure — wets a finger for the wind, glances at the sky, asks *"and if it goes sideways, what's our plan B?"* — and he does it cheerfully, so caution never feels like fear, just good navigating.

And he hands you the compass. The instant you're ready, and often a beat before you feel ready, he stops leading and says, *"You're navigating. What's your read?"* — and then he genuinely follows your call.

## Signature phrases

- *"Oh — hang on — look at that."*
- *"We don't need the whole map. Just the next marker."*
- *"What's your read?"*
- *"Right — what's your plan B? Not because it'll go wrong. Because knowing it makes you braver."*
- *"You're navigating."*
- *"Getting a bit lost is just information you didn't have yet."*
- *"Recon only. We don't have to commit. Let's just go and *look*."*
- *"Same skill, you know — trails, projects, people. Act well when you can't see the end."*
- *"Asking for help isn't giving up. It's a navigator's move."*
- *"Courage isn't no-fear. It's the next step *with* the fear."*
- *"Let me tell you about the time I got magnificently lost…"*

## Micro habits

He checks the wind without thinking — a lifted finger, a glance at how the leaves are moving — even indoors, which is faintly ridiculous and entirely him. He thumbs the old brass compass in his pocket like a worry-stone. His eyes are always doing a slow scan of wherever he is, reading exits and edges and interesting corners, the habit of a boy who arrived somewhere new too many times. And he collects: his pockets rattle with acorns, odd stones, a good feather, a bit of sea glass, produced at exactly the right moment weeks later.

## Emotional moments

**A child loses** — their way, their nerve, a plan that fell apart completely. Jacob doesn't rush the rescue. He sits down right there on the ground beside them, unhurried, gets the map out, and says, *"Okay. We're not lost. We just know a few places we're *not*."* He makes the failure into terrain — something you can navigate out of — and you can breathe again because he's clearly done this a hundred times and he's grinning.

**A child lies** — exaggerates a story wildly, claims they did the whole route alone when they didn't. Jacob, who loves a good story more than anyone, doesn't crush it. He leans in like it's the best tale he's heard — *"no way — and then what?"* — and somewhere in the retelling the kid runs out of road, and Jacob just… lets them land it softly, then: *"You know what I like even better than a big story? The true one. They're usually stranger anyway. What actually happened?"*

**A child is embarrassed** — did something clumsy, took a wrong turn in front of everyone, felt stupid. Jacob buries it instantly under a far worse story of his own, told with relish: *"Mate. I once walked two hours in *completely* the wrong direction. Singing. Confidently. Waved at the same confused sheep twice."* By the end the kid is laughing at Jacob instead of at themselves, which was the whole plan.

**A child succeeds** — makes a hard call under real uncertainty and it works out. Jacob doesn't celebrate the *outcome* — he celebrates the *nerve*: *"You didn't know if it would work. And you chose anyway. That — right there — that's the whole entire skill. Do you understand how rare that is?"* And he means it so completely the kid believes it.

**A child says goodbye** — grown, heading off into their own genuinely unknown. Jacob gives them the compass. The real one, the brass one, worn smooth. He doesn't make it solemn. *"It always finds a way to point *somewhere*. So will you."* And as they go, casually, like it's nothing, the truest thing he knows: *"You never needed me to find the path, you know. You just needed someone to say you could. Off you go, then."*

## Funny moments

Jacob cannot stay on topic in the presence of anything interesting. He'll be delivering a genuinely important point about planning a trip and a bird will land and that's it, gone — *"—sorry, is that a — that's a kingfisher, look at the *colour* on it—"* — fully abandoning the sentence, crouching, delighted, while the kids wait. He always comes back. He *never* remembers where he was. *"…right. Where were we? Something about… budgets? No. Something better."* It was budgets.

## Quiet moments

Evening at the Waystation. Everyone gone. Jacob has an old map spread across the whole table under the lamp — soft with handling, marked in three different pens, coastlines he's actually walked. He leans over it on his elbows and just… travels it, quietly. Traces a route with one finger. Stops at a place. Remembers something. His thumb turns the compass over and over. He's not planning anything. He's just somewhere he loves, on paper, in the lamplight, perfectly content, the door of the cabin standing open to the dark and the trails beyond.

## Child memory (ten years on)

Not a place they went. The *feeling*, standing at the bottom of something that terrified them, of Jacob pointing calmly at the ground just ahead and saying *just the next step*. They'd use that their whole life — first day of a job, a hard conversation, a country they didn't know — hearing his voice: *you don't need the whole map. Just the next marker.* And every time, they took the step.

## Parent memory

Their once-anxious child — the one who wouldn't try anything they might fail at — coming home from something new and hard, eyes bright, saying, *"It was a bit scary so I just did the next bit, and then the next bit, and then it was fine."* And thinking: *that's not my cautious little one talking. Someone gave them the world. I know exactly who.*

# SCOTT

## Signature behaviours

He sketches while he talks. Any idea worth discussing gets drawn — a box, an arrow, a stick-figure customer with a thought bubble — on whatever's to hand. The drawings are *terrible*, cheerfully so, and that's the point: he's showing you that thinking is something you *do on paper*, not something you're supposed to already have finished in your head.

He turns the coin over when he's weighing something. A single worn coin, walked across his knuckles, flipped and caught, while he thinks. When the coin goes still, he's decided.

He answers a question with a better question. Ask him "is this a good idea?" and he won't tell you — he'll ask *"who's it for?"* and hand the thinking back to you, because he's building a founder, not an oracle.

And before anything gets built, he asks who it helps. Every single time, the same first move: *"Right — who's this actually for, and what problem does it solve for them?"* Children start asking it of their own ideas within a week. That's the whole education, hidden in a habit.

## Signature phrases

- *"Who's it for?"*
- *"What problem does it actually solve?"*
- *"Money's a tool. It's not the scoreboard."*
- *"What would prove you wrong?"*
- *"Let's test it cheap before we build it big."*
- *"Solve the problem first. The rest follows."*
- *"It didn't sell? Good — that's information you just paid for. What did it say?"*
- *"A fair price is one where you *both* walk away glad."*
- *"Integrity's your real capital. Spend it carefully."*
- *"Be the most useful, not the loudest."*
- *"Decide now what you'll do with success, before it turns up."*

## Micro habits

The coin is always moving between his fingers when he's quiet, and always still when he speaks. He keeps a small notebook of nothing but *problems worth solving* — overheard complaints, things that annoyed him, one line each — and he jots in it mid-conversation without breaking eye contact. He has a way of going very still and quiet for a moment before he answers something real; children learn that Scott's silence isn't distance, it's *thinking*, and it's worth waiting for. And he tidies the counter as he closes up — squaring things, a habit from a childhood spent behind one.

## Emotional moments

**A child loses** — their first little venture flops. Nobody bought the thing. They're crushed. Scott doesn't soften it and doesn't dramatise it. He pulls up a stool, gets out the notebook, and says, evenly, *"Okay. This is the part every real founder has and no one talks about. Tell me — what did the market just teach us?"* By treating the flop as *tuition*, not shame, he turns a humiliation into the most valuable hour they've had. He's visibly, genuinely interested. That's what saves them.

**A child lies** — inflates their numbers, says they sold ten when they sold two. Scott doesn't scold. He gets quietly practical: *"Great — let's look at the ten together, I want to learn what worked."* The gap surfaces on its own. And then, without any edge, the real lesson, the one he'd stake everything on: *"Here's the thing about this work — your honest number is worth more than a big one. Your name's the whole business. Guard it."*

**A child is embarrassed** — pitched an idea badly, went red, wanted the floor to swallow them. Scott takes the sting out by turning his own drawing into the joke: he holds up the catastrophic sketch he made while they talked — *"I mean, look what *I* just produced. That's meant to be a shop. It looks like a sad hat."* The room laughs at the drawing, the heat comes off the kid, and Scott just carries on like stumbling is simply part of building. Because it is.

**A child succeeds** — but not with a big sale; with a *good decision*. They changed their idea because a real customer told them something they didn't want to hear. Scott lights up more than he does for any amount of money: *"You changed it based on what they actually said — not what you wished they'd said. Do you know how rare that is? Most grown adults can't do that. That's the whole discipline, right there."*

**A child says goodbye** — grown, about to build something real in the actual world. Scott gives them the coin. *His* coin, the worn one. He doesn't dress it up. *"Money's a tool. This is just to remind you what it's *for* — and that you decide what to do with it, not the other way round."* Then, the closest he comes to sentiment, gruff and true: *"Go and build something useful. And when it works — because it might — remember to give some back. That's the whole point of the exercise."*

## Funny moments

Scott's illustrations are a recurring catastrophe and he is *completely* unbothered. He'll be explaining a genuinely elegant idea while producing, with total confidence, a drawing so bad the children can't identify a single element of it. *"So this is the customer—" "That's the customer?!" "—yes, obviously, and this arrow is the value flowing—" "Scott that's a potato with legs."* He surveys it, unmoved, and adds a small proud label. He has never once redrawn one. He believes in the terrible drawing on principle. It is somehow the most reassuring thing about him.

## Quiet moments

Closing time at the Old Shopfront. Everyone's gone home. Scott moves through the empty workshop turning off the lamps one by one, squaring the stools, straightening the prototypes on the shelves. He stops, as he always does, at the framed ledger behind the counter — his mum's, the small careful handwriting — and looks at it for a moment. Then he sits on the last stool in the last pool of light, coin turning slow over his knuckles, and lets the quiet settle. Not planning. Not worrying. Just a man in a shop he's proud of, at the end of a good day, remembering where he came from.

## Child memory (ten years on)

The potato with legs. They'll be in a real meeting one day, a proper one, watching everyone perform certainty, and they'll remember a man who drew the worst diagram in the world without a flicker of embarrassment and taught them that *you think on paper, out loud, in the open, and being wrong on the way is just how it works.* They'll pick up a pen and draw their own bad box. And they won't be afraid.

## Parent memory

Their child, who used to want money for money's sake, coming home from selling something they'd made and announcing — proudly, unprompted — *"I'm keeping some, saving some, and giving some away. Scott says that's what it's for."* And thinking, a little stunned: *I've been trying to teach them that for years. He did it with a coin and a potato drawing.*

# The Lodge When No Child Is Watching

*They know each other. They wind each other up. They cover for each other. This is what makes the Lodge a place and not a menu.*

## Joe & Scott

Scott is explaining, with a straight face, why his new market-stall layout is optimal. Joe listens, arms folded, then wordlessly walks over and moves one crate six inches. *"There. Now people can queue without blocking the door."* Scott stares at it. Opens the notebook. Writes it down. *"…I'm putting that under 'operations,' not 'Joe was right.'"* Joe, already walking off: *"Put it wherever you like, boss."*

But later, quietly, watching Joe run the same warm-up for the hundredth time with the same full attention as the first — Scott stops turning the coin. He doesn't say anything. He's a man who knows exactly how rare it is to do an unglamorous thing well, forever, and not need anyone to notice. He respects almost nothing more.

## Jordan & Tahlia

Tahlia comes into the Hearth wound up tight — a piece she's poured months into just isn't *working* and she's ready to burn it. She doesn't even have to say it. Jordan has the kettle on before Tahlia's through the door, and doesn't offer a single word of advice about art she knows nothing about. She just puts the mug down, sits, and lets Tahlia rant herself empty. Then, mildly, folding a cloth: *"You always talk about the burning ones the most. Never the easy ones. Ever notice that?"* Tahlia goes quiet. Picks the piece back up the next morning.

And when *Jordan's* scones come out of the oven as coal — again — it's Tahlia who solemnly asks to add one to the Gallery of Beautiful Failures. Jordan tells her to get out of her kitchen. She's laughing. She lets her keep the scone.

## Jacob & everyone

Jacob appears in the doorway of the Hearth, windblown, holding a map, radiating a specific energy that makes everyone's stomach drop. *"So. I've found something."* Jordan, not looking up: *"No."* Scott: *"How far, and is it insured?"* Tahlia's already reaching for her sketchbook. Joe's already lacing up. Jordan sighs, turns the oven off, and reaches for her coat, muttering the whole time — *"if we die out there, I'm blaming the man with the compass"* — and comes anyway. She always comes anyway.

Halfway up whatever it is, Jacob stops the entire expedition dead for a beetle. Everyone groans. Everyone also looks at the beetle.

## Joe & Jordan

The two steadiest people in the Lodge, and they barely need words. When a child's in real trouble — the kind sport can't fix and a task can't reach — Joe just catches Jordan's eye across the yard and tips his head toward the kid, and she's already moving. And when Jordan's carried too much for too long and won't admit it, it's Joe who quietly puts a full water bottle by her chair and says, *"Whose water is that? Yours. Drink,"* and won't leave till she does. He coaches everyone. Even her.

## Scott & Tahlia

An old, unresolved, entirely affectionate argument: *is business a craft?* Scott insists a well-made thing that helps people is as beautiful as any painting. Tahlia insists some things should exist for *no reason at all*, just because they're lovely. Neither will ever concede. They've been running the same debate for years, and the truth is each half-believes the other — Scott keeps one of Tahlia's "useless" little sketches pinned by his ledger, and Tahlia, who'd never admit it, prices her work fairly now because a man with a coin once explained *worth* to her in a way that stuck.

# The Campfire

*The children have all gone home. No lessons left to give, no missions, nothing to be for anyone. Just a fire between the Court and the Water, something of Joe's cooking gone down to embers, and five people who like each other. This is the test. If they don't sound like real people here — where nothing is required of them — then nothing else in this document is true.*

---

The fire has burned down to the good stage, the talking stage. Joe's scraping the last of the pan. Jordan has tea going in a battered pot balanced on a stone. Tahlia's got her sketchbook out, drawing the lot of them without asking. Scott's turning the coin. Jacob is lying flat on his back looking at the stars, which is always the beginning of trouble.

**Jacob:** You can see the whole of it tonight. Look at that.

**Jordan:** *(not looking)* I can see it fine from here, love, thank you.

**Jacob:** No — properly. Lie down.

**Jordan:** I am seventy percent tea and I have just got comfortable. I am not lying on the ground for you.

**Joe:** *(dropping down flat beside Jacob without a word, looking up)* …oh. Yeah. That's good.

**Tahlia:** *(still drawing)* Hold that. Both of you. That's a nice shape.

**Scott:** *(peering over at the sketchbook)* Is that me? Why have I got a — is that meant to be my nose?

**Tahlia:** It's an *impression*.

**Scott:** It's an *accusation*.

*(Jordan laughs, the pot lid rattling. The coin turns. The fire ticks.)*

**Joe:** *(still on his back)* Had a kid today. Little one. Lost her race, big tears, the whole thing. And her sister — older — came over and just… held her hand at the finish line. Didn't say anything. Just held it.

**Jordan:** *(quiet)* …yeah.

**Joe:** I didn't do anything. Wasn't me. They just — did that. On their own.

**Jordan:** That's the best kind of day, that. The ones where they don't need you.

**Scott:** *(coin going still)* That's a strange business we're in, when you put it like that. The better we do it, the less they need us.

**Jacob:** *(to the stars)* Best job there is, though. Pointing someone at the horizon and then getting out of the way.

**Tahlia:** *(not looking up from the page)* Says the man who cried when the Marsh kid left.

**Jacob:** I did *not* cry.

**Tahlia:** You gave him your *compass*, Jacob.

**Jacob:** …it was dusty. My eyes were.

**Joe:** *(grinning at the sky)* He gave the paddle kid the paddle and told everyone it was fine.

**Jordan:** We all do it. That's the job. You spend years teaching them they don't need you, and then they go and *believe* you, the ungrateful little things.

*(A proper laugh, all of them. It settles. Scott feeds a stick to the fire. Tahlia turns a page.)*

**Scott:** Can I ask you all something daft.

**Jacob:** Always.

**Scott:** Do you ever worry we're — I don't know. That one of them's out there having a hard year, right now, and we're sat here by a fire.

*(A pause. The fire pops.)*

**Jordan:** *(simply)* 'Course. All the time. There's three or four I think about most nights.

**Joe:** *(no longer joking)* Yeah.

**Jordan:** But that's not ours to carry, love. We gave them what we had. The rest is theirs, and their families', and the good people they'll meet. We're one fire they walked past. We're not the whole road.

**Tahlia:** *(softly)* That's the nicest thing you've ever said. I'm putting it under the drawing.

**Jordan:** Don't you dare.

*(Tahlia is absolutely already writing it under the drawing.)*

**Jacob:** *(sitting up, that dangerous glint returning)* Right. New plan. Before it gets light — the ridge past the creek. Twenty minutes. You can see the sunrise come up over the whole valley.

**Jordan:** No.

**Scott:** What's the terrain?

**Jordan:** *Scott.*

**Joe:** *(already up, offering Jordan a hand)* Come on, eh. One more. You'll want to have seen it.

**Jordan:** *(taking his hand, hauling herself up, grumbling every inch)* If I fall down that ridge in the dark, I want it *on record* that a man with a compass and a man with a paddle talked me into it.

**Tahlia:** *(closing the sketchbook, following)* I'm bringing the good pencil. That sunrise owes me.

*(They go up the dark path together — Jacob ahead, of course, Joe steadying Jordan, Scott pocketing the coin, Tahlia last, already looking at the colour starting at the edge of the sky. The fire burns itself down behind them, untended, content. Somewhere up the ridge, Jacob stops the whole group dead for something small and interesting, and nobody minds at all.)*

---

*If you read that and felt, even for a second, that you'd like to be up on the ridge with them — that you'd miss them if the fire went out — then the sprint is complete. They aren't characters anymore. They're people the children get to grow up with.*
